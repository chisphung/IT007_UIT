# Listening 

## Analysis 

**1. Listening Audio Topics** The Listening section recycles several specific tracks heavily. The most frequently mentioned topics are:

- **Job Applications/Employment Details (9 times):** Tracks involving a candidate applying for a job (e.g., flight attendant, accountant, nanny, or pool lifeguard) or discussing workplace details are the most common Part 1 scenarios.
- **Maori Culture & Hei-Tiki (9 times):** A highly frequent Part 3 academic discussion revolving around Maori rock paintings, New Zealand culture, or the traditional "Hei-Tiki" carving.
- **The History of Gloves (6 times):** A recurring Part 4 lecture detailing the history and usage of gloves.
- **Dolphin Intelligence (4 times):** A Part 4 lecture focusing on dolphin behavior and intelligence.
- **Maple Syrup (3 times):** A Part 4 passage discussing maple syrup production.

**2. Reading Passages** The Reading section shows a strong pattern of recycling specific academic texts. The most frequent passages encountered by candidates include:

- **The Voynich Manuscript (10 times):** This is the single most reported reading passage, frequently described as a difficult Part 3 text involving matching names of researchers and complex vocabulary.
- **Sleep Patterns / Children's Sleep (8 times):** Passages exploring the sleeping habits of hunter-gatherers versus modern humans, or studies addressing sleep problems in children, appeared heavily.
- **The Tasmanian Tiger (7 times):** A very common passage discussing the extinct Tasmanian Tiger, usually featuring gap-filling and matching information.
	- https://realieltsexams.com/san-pham/the-tasmanian-tiger/
- **Chocolate and Health Benefits (6 times):** A passage analyzing the processing, history, and health effects of cocoa/dark chocolate.
- **Flower Power (6 times):** A recurring and notoriously difficult Part 3 passage.
- **Meteorites (5 times):** A frequent Part 2 passage discussing the discovery and market value of meteorites.
- **The History of Pencils (5 times):** A common Part 1 passage detailing the manufacturing and history of pencils.
	- https://realieltsexams.com/san-pham/the-history-of-the-pencil/

**3. Writing Topics** Writing tasks reveal clear trends in the visual data formats tested and the societal themes debated in essays:

- **Task 1 (Visual Data):**
    
    - **Bar Charts (25+ times):** This is by far the most frequent format, asking candidates to compare percentages across categories like employment, energy, or expenditures.
    - **Tables (~24 times):** The second most common format, often requiring candidates to summarize static or dynamic data across multiple countries.
    - **Line Graphs (~18 times):** Frequently testing historical trends and future predictions.
    - **Pie Charts (~16 times):** Often given as "mixed charts" alongside a table or another pie chart.
    - **Maps (~14 times):** Usually asking candidates to compare a town, museum, or park before and after redevelopment.
- **Task 2 (Essays):**
    
    - **Education and School Subjects (~10 times):** Essays debating whether students should focus on a narrow range of subjects vs. a wide range, the role of arts/music in schools, or home education vs. traditional schooling.
    - **Technology and Media (~10 times):** Topics asking if the internet is the most important invention, the decline of handwriting, or the negative/positive impacts of screen time and social media on youth.
    - **Government Control vs. Personal Choice regarding Food/Taxes (8 times):** Essays exploring whether the government should tax unhealthy foods, ban bad products, or subsidize healthy foods.
    - **Globalization and Cultural Sameness (8 times):** Debates on whether countries sharing the same brands, fashions, TV channels, and advertising is a positive or negative development.
    - **Environmental Issues & Fuel Costs (7 times):** The dominant environmental topic asks whether increasing the cost of fuel for vehicles is the best way to solve pollution and climate change.
    - **Road Safety and Driving Tests (6 times):** Debates on whether cyclists or drivers should be forced to take regular driving tests, or if the legal driving age should be increased.

## Difficult analysis

Here are the **Listening tests** that candidates found particularly difficult:

- **Climate Change and Allergies (Part 3):** A reviewer described this section as an absolute nightmare, stating that the difficulty was so high that candidates at a band 5 or 6 level would be completely helpless.
- **The Restoration of Paintings (Part 4):** This audio track caused a shock because the speaking speed suddenly became extremely fast. Furthermore, the vocabulary used was highly unfamiliar and complex.
- **Hospital Services (Part 4):** A candidate found this section "super difficult" because the speaker would state the answer _before_ mentioning the corresponding keyword, causing listeners to miss multiple questions in a row.
- **Arguments For and Against Urban Migration (Part 4):** The speaker's speed was reported to be abnormally fast, requiring extreme concentration to not miss questions.
- **Frog Species (Part 4):** A lecture describing a specific frog's characteristics (such as its "pump system" digestion and inability to live in salt) was highlighted as very hard to follow and hear clearly.
- **Student Projects on Robotics / Museum Projects (Part 3):** Multiple candidates mentioned struggling heavily with Part 3 audios involving student discussions or projects. They reported that the audio presented too much chaotic, overlapping data, making it very difficult to answer the multiple-choice and matching questions.
- **History of Time Measurement & History of Salt (Part 4):** Both of these historical lectures were cited as faster and more difficult than standard practice tests, causing candidates to miss several answers.
