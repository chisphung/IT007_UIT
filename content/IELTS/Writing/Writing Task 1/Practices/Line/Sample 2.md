The line graph illustrates the amount of paper and packaging, sawn-wood, and wood pulp produced by the UK forest industry between 1980 and 2000.

Overall, the production of paper and packaging experienced significant growth, consistently remaining the highest produced category throughout the timeframe. In contrast, the output of sawn-wood declined steadily, while wood pulp production fluctuated slightly but ended at a similar level to where it began.

Looking at the data in detail, paper and packaging production stood at approximately 230 million tonnes (MT) in 1980. This figure saw a moderate increase to 250 MT over the next decade, before surging dramatically to end the period at a peak of 350 MT.

Meanwhile, sawn-wood was the second most produced material in 1980 at 200 MT, but its production experienced a continuous decline, falling to 150 MT by 1990. During the same 10-year period, the volume of wood pulp also decreased slightly from around 170 MT to exactly 150 MT, matching the output of sawn-wood. However, their trends diverged in the final decade: while sawn-wood continued its downward trajectory to hit a low of roughly 130 MT in 2000, wood pulp production recovered, rising to roughly 165 MT.

The line chart illustrates the output of forest industry in three different categories (paper and packaging, sawn-wood, and wood pulp) in the UK, in the years 1980, 1990 and 2000.

Overall, there was an increase in the volume of paper and packaging produced, while sawn-wood's production saw a dramatic decline over the period. The production in paper and packaging accounted for a substantial amount, being the most produced area in the surveyed years.

In 1980, paper and packaging's production started at just about 230 million tonnes (MT), followed by sawn-wood, with about 200 MT. Over the next decade, the former increased slightly to just under 250 MT before reaching a peak at exactly 350 MT at the end of the period,  while the latter declined consistently to 150 MT and approximately 130 MT in 1990 and 2000, respectively. 

Conversely, the production of wood pulp began at roughly 170 MT in 1980; however, the figure dropped slightly to 150 MT in 1990, where it equaled  sawn-wood production. Subsequently, the figure for wood pulp became the second largest productivity among the three categories, recovering to nearly 175 MT at the end of the period.
