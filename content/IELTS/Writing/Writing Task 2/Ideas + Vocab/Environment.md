### **Essential Environment & Space Vocabulary**

**Animals & Conservation**

- **Biodiversity:** The variety of plant and animal life in the world or in a particular habitat.
    
- **Captive breeding:** The process of breeding animals in controlled environments, such as wildlife reserves or zoos.
    
- **Ecological balance / Ecosystem:** A stable state in which all plants and animals in an environment coexist and depend on each other.
    
- **Keystone species:** A species on which other species in an ecosystem largely depend; if it were removed, the ecosystem would change drastically.
    
- **Poaching:** The illegal hunting or capturing of wild animals.
    

**Environment & Resources**

- **Depletion of resources:** The consumption of a resource faster than it can be replenished.
    
- **Sustainable development:** Economic development that is conducted without depletion of natural resources.
    
- **Carbon footprint:** The amount of carbon dioxide and other carbon compounds emitted due to the consumption of fossil fuels by a particular person or group.
    
- **Renewable energy:** Energy from a source that is not depleted when used, such as wind or solar power.
    
- **Habitat destruction / Deforestation:** The clearing of natural habitats, often for agricultural expansion or urban development.
    

**Pollution, Waste & Space**

- **Non-biodegradable:** Substances that cannot be broken down by natural organisms and act as a source of pollution (like most plastics).
    
- **Industrial runoff:** Waste water or chemicals flowing from industrial sites into natural water bodies.
    
- **Extraterrestrial colonization:** The theoretical concept of humans establishing permanent settlements on other planets.
    
- **Astronomical costs:** Extremely high financial expenses (often used to describe space programs).

### **Essay Ideas & Arguments by Sub-Topic**

1. Zoos: Cruel vs. Useful

- **The Cruelty Argument:** Confining wild animals in artificial enclosures causes psychological distress and unnatural behaviors. It deprives them of their natural hunting and ranging instincts.
    
- **The Utility Argument:** Zoos serve as vital sanctuaries for endangered species threatened by poachers or habitat loss. Captive breeding programs help rebuild dwindling populations, and zoos educate the public, fostering a sense of environmental stewardship.
    

2. Protecting Only "Useful" Animals

- **Against this view:** Every animal plays a role in the complex web of an ecosystem. Allowing "useless" predators or insects to die out can cause an overpopulation of pests or a collapse in plant life, eventually harming humans. All species have an intrinsic right to exist.
    
- **For this view (Counter-argument):** In developing nations with limited funds, conservation money might be better spent protecting livestock or pollinator bees that directly support human survival and agriculture.
    

3. Rapid Consumption of Natural Resources

- **Dangers:** Over-reliance on fossil fuels drives severe climate change. Depleting fresh water and arable land leads to famine and geopolitical conflicts (resource wars).
    
- **Solutions:** Governments must subsidize the transition to renewable energy sources. Industries need strict regulations enforcing circular economies (reusing materials), and agriculture must adopt water-efficient technologies like drip irrigation.
    

4. International Cooperation: Environment vs. Business

- **Main Benefit is Environment:** Pollution and climate change do not respect national borders. Global treaties (like the Paris Agreement) are the only effective way to manage ocean conservation and reduce global carbon emissions.
    
- **Main Benefit is Business:** International trade agreements stimulate global economic growth, create jobs, and lift developing nations out of poverty. _Strong thesis:_ While business brings immediate economic relief, environmental cooperation ensures long-term survival, making it the more critical priority.
    

5. Animal Extinction as a "Natural" Process

- **The "Natural" Argument:** Mass extinctions have occurred throughout Earth's history (e.g., the dinosaurs) long before human existence, simply due to planetary changes.
    
- **The "Human Responsibility" Argument:** The current rate of extinction is unnaturally accelerated by human activities, such as industrial pollution, deforestation, and overfishing. Because humanity is the direct cause, we have a moral obligation to prevent it.
    

6. Funding Space Exploration vs. Saving Earth

- **For Space Funding:** Exploring planets like Mars acts as a necessary "backup plan" for human survival in case Earth becomes uninhabitable. Furthermore, space research often yields technological breakthroughs that benefit daily life on Earth (e.g., satellite technology, water purification).
    
- **Against Space Funding:** The billions of dollars spent on space travel would be better utilized solving immediate earthly crises, such as poverty, disease, and climate change. It is far more practical to save our current planet than to try and terraform a dead one.
    

7. Declining Plant and Animal Species

- **Causes:** The primary cause is habitat loss due to aggressive urban expansion and clearing forests for agriculture. Climate change is also altering ecosystems faster than species can adapt.
    
- **Solutions:** Governments must expand protected national parks and wildlife reserves. Internationally, strict bans and heavy penalties must be enforced against the illegal wildlife trade.
    

8. The Lack of Waste Recycling

- **Reasons:** Many cities lack accessible recycling infrastructure, making it inconvenient for residents. Additionally, processing certain plastics is often more expensive for companies than producing new ones.
    
- **Solutions:** Municipalities should introduce "pay-as-you-throw" waste schemes, where residents are charged for general trash but recycling is free, providing a financial incentive. Governments must also run public education campaigns on proper waste sorting.
    

9. Pollution as an Unavoidable Result of Economic Development

- **The "Unavoidable" Argument:** Historically, developing nations rely on cheap, dirty energy (like coal) and relaxed environmental laws to quickly build factories, attract foreign investment, and lift their citizens out of poverty.
    
- **The "Avoidable" Argument:** With modern advancements, developing countries can now "leapfrog" the dirty industrial phase. By investing in green technologies and renewable energy from the beginning—often with the financial help of developed nations—economic growth does not have to equal environmental destruction.
