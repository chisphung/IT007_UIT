### **Essential Work & Employment Vocabulary**

**The Job Market & Competition**

- **Aging workforce:** The trend of older people remaining in the workforce longer.
    
- **Entry-level positions:** Jobs designed for recent graduates or people with little experience.
    
- **Technological obsolescence:** When a person's skills (or a machine) become outdated due to rapid technological changes.
    
- **Digital natives:** Young people who have grown up with modern technology and adapt to it intuitively.
    

**Recruitment & Selection**

- **Unconscious bias:** Prejudices or unsupported judgments that interviewers might hold without realizing it.
    
- **Probationary period:** A trial period for a new employee to prove their skills before becoming permanent.
    
- **Practical competency:** The actual ability to perform the required tasks of a job, rather than just knowing the theory.
    
- **Transferable skills:** Abilities that can be used in many different jobs (e.g., communication, leadership, problem-solving).
    

**Workplace Policies & Well-being**

- **Work-life balance:** The optimal division of one's time and focus between working and family or leisure activities.
    
- **Burnout:** Physical or mental collapse caused by overwork or stress.
    
- **Statutory leave:** The minimum amount of paid holiday time mandated by the government.
    
- **Remuneration / Compensation:** Money paid for work or a service.
    
- **Wealth inequality:** The unequal distribution of assets and income within a society.
    
- **Brain drain:** The emigration of highly trained or intelligent people from a particular country or company.

### **Essay Ideas & Arguments by Sub-Topic**

**1. Older vs. Younger People Competing for Jobs**

- **Problems:** If older people delay retirement, it creates a bottleneck for entry-level positions, leading to high youth unemployment. This can cause social unrest and financial dependence on parents. Furthermore, older workers might struggle to adapt to new technologies, slowing down company innovation.
    
- **Solutions:** Governments should encourage "phased retirement" (working part-time before fully retiring) to open up roles. Companies should implement mutual mentorship programs (older staff teach industry wisdom; younger staff teach digital skills) or invest heavily in youth entrepreneurship grants.
    

**2. Interviews vs. Other Selection Methods**

- **Against Interviews:** They are highly subjective. A candidate with superficial charisma might perform well in an interview but lack actual working skills. Conversely, a highly skilled but introverted candidate might be unfairly rejected due to nerves.
    
- **Better Alternatives:** Practical assessments, portfolio reviews, or a one-month probationary period are far more reliable. These methods test a candidate's actual competency and how they function in a real workplace environment.
    

**3. Job-Hopping Among Young People**

- **Reasons:** Young professionals frequently change jobs to secure faster salary increases, escape toxic work cultures, or gain diverse experiences. The modern gig economy has normalized short-term loyalty.
    
- **Advantages:** The employee builds a vast, adaptable skill set and a wide professional network. They become highly resilient to market changes.
    
- **Disadvantages:** Companies are reluctant to invest in training employees who might leave in a year. For the employee, a resume full of short stints can eventually create a "job hopper stigma," making them look unreliable to future employers.
    

**4. A Satisfying Career as the Most Important Element of Life**

- **For this view:** Adults spend roughly a third of their lives at work. A fulfilling career provides financial security, a sense of identity, intellectual stimulation, and personal self-worth.
    
- **Against this view (Counter-argument):** While work is important, prioritizing it above all else leads to burnout and broken relationships. Health, family, and personal passions are arguably more fundamental to a person's overall happiness. A job should be viewed as a means to fund a fulfilling life, not the entirety of life itself.
    

**5. Restricting Extremely High Salaries**

- **For Restrictions (Salary Caps):** Capping executive pay reduces extreme wealth inequality. The surplus capital could be redistributed to increase the wages of the lowest-paid workers or taxed to fund public services like healthcare and education.
    
- **Against Restrictions:** Imposing a cap removes the financial incentive to innovate or take on massive responsibilities (like being a CEO or a top surgeon). It would also cause a "brain drain," where top talent simply emigrates to countries with a free-market economy where they can earn their true market value.
    

**6. Mandatory Four-Week Holidays for Better Performance**

- **For Mandatory Holidays:** Continuous work leads to diminishing returns and burnout. Extended time off allows employees to mentally reset, spend time with family, and return to work with higher productivity, creativity, and morale. This ultimately lowers staff turnover.
    
- **Against (Counter-argument):** A blanket rule might cripple small businesses or start-ups that cannot afford to lose staff for a month. It disrupts project momentum in highly competitive industries.
    

**7. Laws Limiting Working Hours**

- **For Limits:** Strict labor laws protect vulnerable workers from corporate exploitation and unpaid overtime. By capping hours, governments actively safeguard public health, reducing stress-related illnesses and ensuring parents have time to raise their children.
    
- **Against Limits:** Some individuals _want_ to work extra hours to maximize their earning potential or advance quickly in their careers. Strict limits infringe on personal freedom and can stall rapid economic development in emerging markets.
