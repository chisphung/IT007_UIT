### **Essential Crime & Law Vocabulary**

**The Justice System & Punishment**

- **Incarceration:** The state of being confined in prison.
    
- **Rehabilitation:** The process of restoring someone to health or normal life through training and therapy after imprisonment.
    
- **Punitive measures:** Actions intended to inflict punishment rather than to heal or educate.
    
- **Deterrent:** A thing that discourages or is intended to discourage someone from committing a crime (e.g., "Strict laws act as a deterrent").
    
- **Community service:** Unpaid work performed by a person for the benefit of their community, often as a form of alternative sentencing.
    

**Juvenile Crime**

- **Juvenile delinquency:** The habitual committing of criminal acts or offenses by a young person.
    
- **Impressionable:** Easily influenced because of a lack of critical ability (often applied to youth).
    
- **Peer pressure:** Influence from members of one's peer group, which can lead to negative behaviors.
    
- **Formative years:** The early years of a person's life when their character and personality are being shaped.
    

**Reoffending & Society**

- **Recidivism / Recidivism rate:** The tendency of a convicted criminal to reoffend.
    
- **Reintegration:** The action or process of integrating an ex-offender back into society.
    
- **Societal stigma:** A mark of disgrace associated with a particular circumstance, quality, or person (e.g., the stigma of a criminal record).
    
- **Root causes:** The fundamental, underlying reasons for a problem (such as poverty or lack of education driving crime).

### **Essay Ideas & Arguments by Sub-Topic**

**1. Education as a Better Solution than Prison**

- **For Education:** Crime is often a symptom of extreme poverty and a lack of economic opportunity. Providing high-quality education gives individuals the skills needed to secure legal employment, thereby addressing the _root cause_ of crime. Furthermore, prisons often act as "schools for criminals," where petty thieves learn worse behaviors from hardened inmates.
    
- **For Prison (The Counter-argument):** While education is an excellent preventative measure, it is not a cure-all. Prison is absolutely necessary to remove violent or dangerous individuals from society to ensure public safety. For severe crimes, punitive measures and the deprivation of freedom are required to deliver justice to victims.
    

**2. Reasons for Juvenile Crime & How to Punish It**

- **Reasons:** The rise in juvenile crime can often be linked to a breakdown in family structures, such as both parents working long hours and leaving children without supervision. Youth are also highly impressionable; constant exposure to violence in media or intense peer pressure can normalize illegal behavior.
    
- **How They Should Be Punished:** The focus for teenagers should heavily favor _rehabilitation_ over incarceration. Sending a teenager to an adult prison destroys their future and hardens them. Instead, they should face mandatory counseling, educational programs, and community service, which teaches them responsibility and empathy without permanently ruining their lives.
    

**3. Criminals Reoffending After Release (Recidivism)**

- **The Problem / Reasons:** When prisoners are released, they carry the heavy societal stigma of a criminal record, making it nearly impossible for them to find lawful employment or secure housing. If the prison system only focused on punishment rather than rehabilitation, the ex-convict leaves with no new skills. Faced with starvation or homelessness, they are quickly forced back into their old criminal networks to survive.
    
- **Potential Solutions:** Prisons must be transformed into rehabilitative centers. Inmates should be required to complete vocational training (e.g., carpentry, plumbing, coding) and psychological counseling while serving their sentences. Upon release, the government should provide halfway houses for a gradual transition and offer tax incentives to companies that are willing to hire ex-offenders, allowing them to rebuild their lives legally.
